## What is Financial Modeling?

### A Company: current stock price is $150 per share

* Argument with Your Friend: Friend thinks the company is worth $100 per share, but you think it’s worth $200 per share
* You: Recommend investing, but your friend recommends “shorting” (betting against the company)
* How can you tell who’s correct?
* Answer: Create a “financial model” to quantify your views



### Financial Models: Similar to outlines for long essays or blueprints for buildings – not all the details, but enough to make a decision

* This Company: Has grown sales at 10% per year for a few years
* Your Financial Model: Says that for the company to be worth $200 per share, it must grow sales at 30% per year
* Friend’s Model: For the company to be worth $100 per share, it must only grow sales at 5% per year
* RESULT: Your friend is more likely to be correct because 10% -> 5% growth is more plausible than 10% -> 30% growth



### “Financial modeling” lets you quantify your views of a company

* Use Cases: Stock market investing, advising on mergers and acquisitions, acquiring companies in leveraged buyouts, or raising capital
* Example 1: A pharmaceutical company’s stock price just fell by 50% – should you invest? Or will the price keep falling?
* Example 2: A retailer wants to acquire a smaller retailer – should it?
* Role: Similar to DNA evidence or witnesses in a courtroom trial – it’s part of the process, but it doesn’t dictate the entire outcome



### So, What is finacial modeling?

**It's a way to quantify your views about company or a deal and assign numbers to your view of that company or deal** 

So, if you're having an argument about what a company is really worth, you can create a financial model and see if you're right or if your friend is more likely to be correct about the company's value based on the assumptions that you make. It's similar to evidence, such as DNA evidence or witnesses in a courtroom trial. It doesn't determine who wins the trial, but it contributes to one side's argument and it helps that side win the trial.



# The Steps in the Financial Modeling Process

---

### Step 1: Pick Your Purpose

* Stock Pitch: **Valuation** tends to be most important
* Metrics and Ratios: Financial statement projections
* Merger/Acquisition: Valuation, but also a **merger model**
* Buy Entire Company: **Leveraged buyout model** is critical
* Commonalities: Need revenue, expenses, and future cash flows
* **Time Spent: 5 minutes, hopefully**



### Step 2: Do Some Background Research

* Start With: Company’s interim and annual reports
* Then: Investor presentations, outside industry research
* If You Can: “Equity research” on company, issued by banks
* Bonus Points: Interview management or do “channel checks”
* Private Co’s: Need to get info directly from them
* **Time Spent: Might be 1 hour, or might be days/weeks+**



### Step 3: Identify the Key Drivers

* Retailer: # of stores, sales per store, costs per product sold
* Food & Beverage: Units sold, average price, cost per unit
* SaaS Company: Monthly fee, # customers, cancellation rate
  * Software as a Service: a software delivery model where applications are hosted by a vendor and accessed over the internet via subscriptions, eliminating local installation and maintenance.
  * Common examples include Google Workspace, Salesforce, Slack, Netflix, and Zoom.
* Hotel: # hotels, # rooms, occupancy rate, room rate, costs per hotel
* Pharma Co: # patients, price per patient, R&D and sales
* **Time Spent: 30-60 minutes?**



### Step 4: Gather Data on Other Companies(if it's required)

* **Tricky: Varies A LOT based on the analysis and required precision**
* Extreme Case: “Fairness Opinion” in investment banking
  * Fairness Opinion is basically a very detailed valuation of a company where you go through hundreds or thousands of pages of companies’ filings and you make very, very tiny adjustments to get everything exactly correct.
* Other Extreme: Quick analysis to see if a buyout works
* Valuation: Tends to be VERY data-intensive – need similar co’s
* M&A: Less so, but need information on multiple companies now
* **Time Spent: 1 hour up to days or weeks, depending on the context**



### Step 5: Build the Analysis

* Always: Revenue and expenses for your company
* Always: Full or partial “financial statements”
* Sometimes: Valuation – need lots of outside data + adjustments
* Sometimes: Acquisitions – more about scenarios with your data
* Rarely: Extreme precision down to individual employees
* **Time Spent: 30 minutes up to… 3 months? Yes, sometimes!**



### Step 6: Present Your Conclusions

* What: Buy the company? Fund the project? Sell the stock?
* Why: Supporting reasons, backed by numbers
* How: What’s the best structure / timing?
* Format: PowerPoints slides, Word document, oral presentation
* Audience: Senior bankers, PM, Partners, interviewers, or clients
* **Time Spent: Several hours up to 1 week+**
