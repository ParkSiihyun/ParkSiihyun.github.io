BIWS Brian
June 30, 2025 2:59am

Yashvardhan,

The real estate market in Korea, at least in Seoul, has arguably been in a bubble for a long time (decades?) because of the prevalence of Option #1 (Jeon-se). People essentially get money upfront from their tenants and then use it to buy other real estate and then hope the price of that real estate goes up.

And then they find tenants for those units, get their money, invest it in more/bigger units, and hope for price appreciation on those as well.

It makes the entire system dependent on rising prices and people continuing to pay huge amounts upfront, so I don't think it's going to end well with the declining population there.

If prices ever fall substantially, the entire system comes crashing down because landlords won't be able to repay their tenants' deposits when they move out (they might be able to, but they'd have to accept losses on other properties they sell to get the cash for this).

With option #2 here, yes, if you had $150K, you would have to spend some of it on rent, but we're counting the rent payments separately and assuming they come from your ongoing salary or paycheck.

So the relevant question is whether you should invest the $140K *extra* as a result of picking option #2 or whether you should put all $150K into the apartment all as of today's date.

REPLY
BIWS Brian
May 18, 2024 11:31pm

Bryan,

At 8:10 here, we calculate the Lost Earnings in Year 2 by taking the $140 Deposit Difference, adding the Lost Earnings in Year 1, and then multiplying all that by the 10% opportunity cost.

It's ($15) rather than ($14) because by Year 2, we haven't just given up the 10% we could have earned on the $140 greater Deposit - we've also lost the 10% we could have earned on the $14 in lost earnings from Year 1.

So, instead of losing $14, as in Year 1, we lose more like $14 + $1.4 = $15.4, which shows up as $15 here due to rounding.
